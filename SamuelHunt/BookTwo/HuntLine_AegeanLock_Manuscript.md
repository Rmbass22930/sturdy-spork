### 1. Return to Athens
The tracker Leyla had sewn into Maverick’s luggage blinked alive two blocks from the Acropolis Museum. Samuel Hunt leaned against a graffiti-tagged vespa, watching tourists shuffle past as if history weren’t loading guns in the alleys. His phone vibrated with another ping, triangulating the signal inside a penthouse overlooking Plaka. Greek National Intelligence Service watchers—cheap suits, better shoes—had clocked him minutes ago. They spread out like chess pawns; Hunt smiled politely, kept his hands visible. No point spooking allies he hadn’t met yet.

He rode a battered scooter through winding streets, parking beside an olive tree whose roots split ancient stones. He climbed four flights, kicked in the penthouse door, and found no Maverick—only a satchel containing printed manifests stamped **HELLENIC NAVY – AEGEAN LOCK**. Someone wanted him to see it. He scanned the pages, saw references to decommissioned NATO missile silos on Karpathos and warehouse deliveries in Piraeus. A trap is still intel, he told himself.

Two men burst in, guns drawn, shouting in Greek. Hunt raised his hands, replied in their language that he was delivering a warning. That’s when Daphne Markou entered. Late twenties, black bob, glasses hiding sharp eyes. “He’s the American ghost,” she said in English. “The one Maverick blames for everything.” She dismissed the agents with a flick. “I’m Daphne, NIS Cyber Division. We flagged your tracker. If you’re here to burn Athens, we’ll bury you. If not, tell me why Maverick is using our naval stamps.”

Hunt handed her the manifests. “Because he wants the EU to believe Greece is building its own nukes,” he said. “And because Talal’s money funneled through your banks.”

Daphne scanned the papers, cursed softly. “Aegean Lock is a real project. Decommissioned bases scheduled for conversion into data centers. Someone’s hijacked the paperwork.” She looked up. “I can get you into NIS archives—for a favor. My director thinks Maverick’s propaganda proves we should arrest you. I need leverage to prove him wrong.”

“What’s the favor?”

“Find out why Maverick is meeting a Russian oligarch in Piraeus tonight. Bring me something I can’t ignore.” She held out her hand. Hunt shook it. Allies in Athens were transactional; he could live with that.

He stepped onto the balcony, scanning the city. The Parthenon glowed amber against the dusk. Somewhere in those ruins, empires swore their gods would protect them. Hunt knew better. Only preparation did. He texted Leyla: **Athens hot. NIS ally—conditional. Prepare for port op.**

Her reply came fast: **Already on the docks. Bring baklava.**

Hunt grinned. He pocketed the manifests, slid down the fire escape, and disappeared into Plaka’s maze. Athens wasn’t home, but tonight it felt like familiar ground—full of history, lies, and opportunity.

### 2. Agora Intercept
Monastiraki Square smelled of souvlaki and counterfeit leather. Leyla blended with tourists, sunglasses hiding her gaze, a tray of baklava in hand. Hunt moved through the flea market, eyes scanning stalls stacked with vinyl records and icons. Daphne monitored from a rooftop, laptop balanced on a milk crate. “Maverick’s courier just hit your sector,” she whispered through Hunt’s earpiece. “White shirt, blue cap, carrying a tote marked ‘AEGEAN SEA SALT.’”

Hunt spotted him weaving between tables. Leyla sidestepped, “accidentally” smearing honey on the man’s sleeve. “Efharisto,” she cooed, slipping a slim GPS bead into the tote seam. The courier apologized, hurried toward the metro. Hunt followed at a distance, letting Daphne’s drone track overhead.

They tailed him down to the green line, boarded separate cars. The courier met a clean-cut man wearing a clerical collar—odd choice. He passed the tote; the priest whispered a blessing and exited at Omonia. Hunt followed, only to find the man slip into a side door of an Orthodox bookshop. Inside, stacks of religious texts hid a server rack humming in the back. The “priest” discarded his collar, revealing a Mossad agent Hunt recognized: Yael Ben-Ami, Samir’s handler.

“You’re a long way from Tel Aviv,” Hunt said quietly. Yael didn’t flinch. “So are you. I’m here for Samir. Mossad wants him alive to explain why he stole our reactor. Maverick promised to deliver him if we provide safe passage through Greek waters. I said no. Now Maverick is bribing rogue naval officers to do it.”

Hunt considered. “Samir fell off a train. If he’s alive, he’s working for Maverick. Help me stop Aegean Lock, I’ll give you whatever remains of Samir.”

Yael eyed him. “I also need proof Talal funded this. My government wants leverage on Riyadh.” She handed him a flash drive. “Interrogation logs from Mossad assets tied to Lev Orlov. Maybe it buys me your trust.”

Hunt slipped it into his pocket. “Daphne will love this.” He left the shop, reconvened with Leyla. “We have a Mossad truce,” he said. Leyla rolled her eyes. “Again? Your friendships are weird.”

Back at the square, Noura and Dr. Aris Papadopoulos arrived, arms full of notebooks. Aris wore a tweed jacket despite the heat, eyes alight. “I heard Cedar Crown reached Greece,” he said. “If they’re using our islands as batteries, we need to design counter-sabotage.”

Hunt nodded. “Welcome to the team. Hope you like ferries.” He looked toward the port skyline, cranes silhouetted against the setting sun. Tonight they’d go to Piraeus. The war had moved to the sea.

### 3. Piraeus Whisper
Piraeus after midnight glowed with sodium lights and offloaded dreams. Container stacks towered like miniature cities. Hunt, Leyla, and Jenny Stavros—arriving on a motorcycle with salty hair and quick grin—slipped past a dozing security guard using Daphne’s cloned badge.

“Jenny owes me,” Leyla explained. “She smuggled Cycladic statues for a Russian collector who never paid.”

Jenny spat on the concrete. “If that collector is Lev Orlov, I’m in.” She produced a set of lock picks fashioned from sailboat rigging. Within minutes, they cracked a container labeled **Aegean Lock – Telecommunications Equipment**. Inside: modular reactor components packed in foam, each stamped with a NATO inventory number.

Hunt photographed everything. Daphne piped in. “Greek Navy’s logistics system shows these shipments heading to Karpathos under Cmdr. Nikos Vartholomeos’s authority. He’s supposed to be decommissioning old radar sites. Instead, he’s building micro-reactor nests.”

Jenny placed a palm on the metal. “These would fit perfectly inside old missile silos,” she said. “If they overheat, the blast vents could turn Cyclades into a fallout chain.”

Leyla planted micro-camera beacons under the pallet, feeding live video to Maya. Hunt rigged a small thermite charge under the container door—not to detonate now, but to slice the hinges remotely later. “Insurance,” he muttered.

Footsteps echoed. Hunt motioned for silence. Two men in naval uniforms approached, discussing payoffs in hushed Greek. One mentioned meeting “Orlov’s broker” at the fish market. The other bragged about his new villa on Mykonos. Leyla smirked. “We should crash that party.”

They slipped out before being spotted. On the dock, Hunt met Daphne, who held up her tablet showing bank transfers between Orlov’s shell companies and Nikos’s accounts. “This is enough to get a warrant,” she said. “But my director still suspects you.”

Hunt held up Yael’s flash drive. “Then let’s give him a show. Next stop, Mt. Athos—where Maverick launders his conscience through monks.”

Jenny groaned. “Great. I love being banished from holy places.” Hunt grinned. “You’re not banished. Just uninvited.”

### 4. Mt. Athos Firewall
Mount Athos wasn’t a hacker hub; it was a peninsula of monasteries where women weren’t allowed and monks guarded centuries of scripture. Yet somewhere within its stone walls, Maverick had hidden a data haven. Maya traced satphone bursts to one monastery perched above the Aegean. Hunt and Daphne ferried over on a fishing boat, disguising their gear as donations. Leyla had to sit this one out, fuming on the mainland. Jenny delivered the boat with a wink. “Bring me a relic,” she joked.

The abbot greeted them with wary politeness. Daphne presented forged letters from the Greek Ministry of Culture, citing digitization assistance. Inside, they saw rows of servers tucked between icons—a sacrilegious juxtaposition. Maverick had paid for the monastery’s new solar array, then slipped in a data bunker.

Hunt and Daphne set up “scan equipment,” actually deploying a worm onto the servers. The worm accessed offshore accounts tied to Cedar Crown. Maya guided them remotely, voice hushed. “Nice work. These nodes connect Orlov’s cryptocurrency wallets to Nikos’s accounts and to a Russian dark fleet vessel anchored near Mykonos.”

Monks began chanting in the chapel. Daphne glanced at Hunt. “We have ten minutes before vespers end and they ask questions.” Hunt nodded. He planted a passive tap to keep feeding data to Maya.

As they packed up, the abbot approached. “You are not who you say you are,” he murmured. “But I sense you fight evil.” He pressed a wooden prayer bead bracelet into Hunt’s palm. “Take this. When conscience burns, remember humility.”

Hunt swallowed. “Efharisto.” Outside, the sea glittered, storms brewing. On the boat back, Daphne debriefed. “We now have proof of Orlov’s financing and Maverick’s monastery laundering. But my director still suspects you.”

“Then give him something he can’t ignore,” Hunt said. “Bring him to the museum gala tonight. We’ll steal a naval badge under his nose and show him how deep the rot goes.” Daphne laughed. “You’re insane.” Hunt grinned. “Frequently.”

### 5. Jenny’s Debt
The National Archaeological Museum glittered under gala lights. Wealthy patrons in tuxedos and gowns sipped champagne while antiquities glowed behind glass. Hunt wore a black suit Leyla insisted he buy. “If you’re going to crash Greek high society, at least look better than the statues,” she’d said.

Jenny Stavros arrived in a borrowed gown, tattoos hidden beneath silk gloves. She eyed a naval officer near the bronze collection. “That’s Cmdr. Nikos,” she whispered. “He owes me for a yacht he impounded.” Leyla smirked. “Let’s collect.”

Their plan unfolded in layers. Noura gave a TED-style talk on nuclear ethics to distract donors. Daphne introduced Director Gatzos to Hunt, who fed him doctored intel hinting that Nikos planned to sell missiles. Meanwhile, Jenny flirted with Nikos, luring him onto the balcony overlooking the courtyard. Leyla spiked his drink with a mild sedative—just enough to slow reflexes. When Nikos leaned over the railing to whisper sweet bribery, Hunt brushed past, lifting his access badge with a magician’s finesse.

Nikos staggered back inside, oblivious. Jenny pocketed the badge, winked at Hunt. “Debt settled.” She then planted Leyla’s GPS earring on Nikos’s lapel. “Now we can track him when he sails.”

Inside, Noura’s speech drew applause. She announced the Cedar Coalition Athens chapter, urging donors to fund transparent energy research. The message undercut Maverick’s fear narrative. Daphne whispered to Gatzos, showing him Orlov’s ledgers on her tablet. His face paled. “If this is true, we have traitors in the navy,” he said. “Arrest them quietly. And as for the American—”

Hunt raised an eyebrow. “Yes?”

“—don’t leave Athens,” Gatzos finished. “Unless you’re going to Crete to stop a war.” He handed Hunt a temporary clearance card. “Make sure you come back.”

After the gala, the team regrouped on a rooftop bar. Jenny toasted with ouzo. “To stealing from traitors,” she said. Hunt clinked glasses. “To making new ones nervous.”

Leyla checked her tablet. “Nikos just boarded a patrol craft headed for Karpathos. Our tracker’s live.” Hunt stood. “Then Act II begins. Pack for storms.” He looked around the table—Leyla, Noura, Daphne, Jenny, Aris. A new coalition, forged in jazz bars and monasteries. “Greece deserves better than Maverick’s games,” he said. “Let’s rewrite his rules.”

They nodded. Below, Athens buzzed oblivious. Above, the Parthenon watched silently, as it had watched empires rise and fall. Hunt tightened the prayer beads around his wrist. Tomorrow, they’d sail into Cycladic winds. Tonight, they planned—and tried, briefly, to breathe.

### 6. Karpathos Drift
Karpathos looked peaceful from the ferry—whitewashed houses clinging to hillsides, fishermen mending nets, tourists photographing goats. Hunt knew better. Beneath the cliffs lay a decommissioned NATO radar base now guarded by Cmdr. Nikos Vartholomeos’s men. Jenny sailed them ashore on a vintage dinghy, cutting the engine to glide silently into a cove.

Hunt, Leyla, Jenny, and Aris hiked up a goat path, avoiding patrol drones. Daphne fed them live satellite imagery. “Nikos’s patrol craft docked thirty minutes ago,” she said. “He brought four crates labeled ‘Medical Aid.’ Thermal shows dense cores—likely micro-reactor housings.” Noura and Barakat remained offshore on Jenny’s larger sailboat, ready to extract or provide comms.

They reached the base perimeter as wind whipped the cliff. Hunt spotted a rusted chain-link fence patched with modern sensors. He used the EMP pen Leyla had saved to fry a section quietly. “We only have one of those,” she whispered. “Don’t waste it on goats next time.” He smirked.

Inside, the base’s main bunker door bore NATO insignia faded by salt. Nikos’s men lounged nearby, smoking. Jenny tossed a pebble down the cliff. The guards looked away at the sound. Hunt and Leyla slipped past, pressed themselves against the concrete entrance. Jenny and Aris knocked out sensors with adhesive squares. In minutes, they were inside.

The bunker still smelled of hydraulic oil and cold war paranoia. In the central chamber, they found micro-reactor modules assembled around the old missile tubes. Engineers in civilian clothes calibrated systems under Nikos’s supervision. Hunt recognized one engineer from the Piraeus surveillance feed—a Russian wearing civilian coveralls, likely Orlov’s man.

Leyla whispered, “We can’t fight them all.” Hunt nodded. “We don’t need to. We need proof.” He planted micro-cameras and a timed virus on the control terminals. Aris adjusted a coolant valve, introducing a micro-delay. “If they power this up, the auto-shutdown will trigger and send a warning to NATO servers,” he said. “Enough to force a full review.”

Nikos stepped away to take a call. Jenny used the distraction to slip the tracking earring from his lapel, replacing it with a dummy. “He upgraded to a Faraday pouch,” she murmured. “Think he learned from last time.”

As they withdrew, Aris left a sensor tuned to detect Orlov’s unique fuel signature. “Now Maya can track any other shipments using this isotope blend,” he said. Hunt clapped his shoulder. “Remind me to never play chess against you.”

They exfiltrated the way they came, barely avoiding a drone sweep by diving into a drainage ditch. Back on the dinghy, Jenny grinned. “Next time, can we infiltrate somewhere sunny minus the missiles?” Hunt laughed. “This was sunny.”

On the sailboat, Noura processed the data. “This reactor design is different,” she said. “Maverick reinforced the containment to survive missile strikes. He wants these to endure even if NATO attacks.”

Hunt stared at the horizon. “Then we make sure they never activate.” He radioed Daphne. “Karpathos confirmed. Prepare warrants for Nikos and Orlov. We’ll need the Navy’s help, even if they hate us.”

“Copy,” Daphne said. “Meanwhile, you, Leyla, and Jenny have a museum gala to crash—and a badge to leverage. Nikos will be there in twelve hours, showing off his donations.”

“We already stole his badge,” Leyla said.

“Steal his donors next,” Daphne replied. “They fund his schemes.”

### 7. Temple Run
The Archaeological Museum gala had barely ended when the team turned its spoils into leverage. Daphne convinced Director Gatzos to authorize a quiet arrest of Nikos—if they could catch him taking a bribe. Maya located the bribe: a secret meeting at the Temple of Poseidon at Cape Sounion, where Orlov’s broker would hand Nikos a duffel of euros.

Leyla wore her “gallery curator” disguise, Jenny pretended to be a yacht heiress, and Hunt played the role of security consultant. They arrived at sunset, the temple glowing gold. Tourists snapped photos unaware of the sting unfolding.

Nikos waited near the cliffs, duffel by his feet. Orlov’s broker, a slick Greek-Russian named Petros, arrived with a briefcase. Hunt, hidden behind a column, listened through a parabolic mic as Nikos bragged about placing reactors “where even Poseidon can’t drown them.” Petros warned him: “Orlov expects results, and Maverick expects secrecy. If NIS sniff around, you deal with them.”

Leyla gave the signal. Daphne’s NIS arrest team moved in, surrounding Nikos. Gatzos himself stepped from the shadows, badge displayed. “Commander, you’re under arrest for treason.” Nikos went for his pistol; Hunt disarmed him with a strike to the wrist. Petros tried to run. Jenny tripped him with a sailing rope she’d brought, of course.

As NIS hauled them away, Nikos sneered at Hunt. “You’re just a mercenary,” he hissed. Hunt shrugged. “Mercenaries get paid. I do this for free.”

Later, on the drive back, Gatzos thanked Hunt begrudgingly. “You saved us embarrassment,” he said. “But the Navy still resents foreign meddling.” Hunt replied, “Then let’s give them sovereign credit. Leak Nikos’s arrest as an NIS success. We stay ghosts.”

Leyla grinned. “Look at you, playing politics.” Hunt groaned. “Don’t get used to it.”

### 8. Cyclone over Milos
With Nikos arrested, Orlov activated his contingency: move the remaining reactors to Milos during a predicted meltemi storm, betting that bad weather would keep surveillance away. Jenny’s sailing skills became essential. She piloted Hunt, Leyla, and Daphne through churning seas, lightning cracking overhead.

Radar showed Orlov’s convoy—a stealth barge escorted by speedboats with Russian mercenaries. Hunt’s plan: board the barge mid-storm, plant thermite on the reactor crates, then bail before the explosives cooked off. Leyla called it insane. Jenny called it Tuesday.

They approached under cover of waves, grappling onto the barge as rain blinded the mercs. Hunt fought in slippery chaos, using elbows and knees in true Reacher fashion. Leyla deployed smoke grenades that mixed with rain to create impenetrable fog. Daphne jammed comms, preventing Orlov from coordinating.

Hunt slapped thermite charges on the crates, set to slow burn so evidence could be gathered after they sank. He radioed Maya: “Thermite planted. Get Greek coast guard to ‘discover’ the wreckage so NATO has proof.”

As they leapt back to Jenny’s boat, a cyclone gust nearly capsized them. Jenny cursed, steering through the troughs. Hunt held onto the mast, muscles straining. “This is worse than Baghdad,” he shouted. Jenny laughed maniacally. “This is Greece, honey!”

Behind them, the barge exploded, flames searing the rain. Orlov’s speedboats retreated. Jenny guided them to a hidden cove where Barakat waited with towels and sarcastic comments. “You looked wetter than cats,” he said. Leyla flipped him off, grinning.

Maya confirmed the coast guard recovered debris containing reactor components. EU media exploded with news of “mysterious military cargo” washing up. Talal’s financiers panicked. Orlov issued a statement denying involvement, which no one believed.

“Next, Santorini,” Hunt said. “If Maverick wants to use volcanoes as power plants, we’ll turn them against him.”

### 9. Volcano Vault
Santorini’s caldera resonated with geothermal power—and newly installed Cedar Crown relay nodes disguised as tourist infrastructure. Noura and Dr. Aris led this op, with Hunt and Leyla providing security. They descended into a restricted maintenance tunnel beneath the island’s cable car system, bypassing Greek guards with forged permits.

Inside, they found a control room connected to the island’s geothermal plant. Noura hacked the SCADA system, discovering code signatures matching Maverick’s work. “He’s rerouting the plant to act as a backup battery for the micro-reactors,” she said. “If one fails, Santorini feeds it. If Santorini overloads, you get a volcanic steam explosion aimed at cruise ships.”

Aris proposed a counter: reprogram the plant to divert excess power into the sea, creating thermal blooms that NATO satellites could spot—a signature exposing every reactor linked to the network. Hunt guarded the corridor while they worked. Leyla planted C4 on the relay nodes as insurance.

Halfway through, Mossad agent Yael arrived unannounced, pistol drawn. “I’m here for Samir,” she said. “Our intel says he’s hiding in Santorini, working for Maverick.” Hunt gritted his teeth. “You pick bad times.”

Yael smirked. “You owe me. I helped expose Talal.” She handed over a thumb drive containing Lev Orlov’s yacht itinerary. “Take this, let me hunt Samir.”

Hunt compromised. “Finish your search quietly. If you find him, bring him alive. Meanwhile, help us defend this tunnel.” Yael rolled her eyes but complied, securing the entrance.

Noura and Aris completed the reroute, smiling tiredly. “The Cedar Coalition just saved half the Aegean,” Aris said. Leyla triggered the C4, blowing the relay nodes. The explosion rocked the tunnel but didn’t damage the plant.

Above ground, tourists continued sipping wine, oblivious. Hunt looked over the caldera. “Volcano stable,” he reported to Maya. “But Maverick won’t like losing his battery.”

“He already moved to Delphi,” she replied. “He’s hosting a live broadcast there, twisting Greek mythology to paint you as a villain. Time to crash the show.”

### 10. Delphi Echoes
Delphi’s ruins echoed with history and—thanks to Maverick—propaganda. He’d set up a holographic projection near the Temple of Apollo, broadcasting a speech about “foreign ghosts stealing Greek sovereignty.” Greek media streamed it live. NIS Director Gatzos, pressured by public opinion, wavered.

Hunt arrived with Leyla, Daphne, and Jenny. They moved through olive groves, avoiding cameras. Maya hacked the signal, but Maverick had air-gapped the projector. Hunt chose the Reacher solution: walk on stage and punch the villain. As he approached, a sniper fired. Hunt dove behind a column. “Maverick’s turned locals into hit squads,” Leyla muttered.

Jenny spotted the sniper—an NIS officer turned by Maverick. Daphne confronted him, using her badge and a gut-punch to disarm him. Meanwhile, Hunt charged the stage, smashed the projector with a crowbar, and grabbed Maverick’s portable broadcast unit. Maverick, appearing via hologram, smirked. “You can destroy toys, Sam, but the narrative sticks. They believe I defend them.”

Hunt held up his phone, streaming Karpathos and Milos evidence onto the same screens Maverick used. The crowd gasped seeing Cmdr. Nikos receiving bribes, Orlov’s shipments, and Maverick gloating. “Truth beats narrative,” Hunt said. “Especially live.”

An NIS SWAT team, tipped by Daphne, moved in to secure the area. Protesters threw rocks at Maverick’s supporters. Greek social feeds flipped from #MaverickSavesGreece to #AegeanLock. Gatzos called Hunt. “Fine,” he said. “I believe you. Take my authorization and hit Crete. Maverick’s partners are regrouping at Souda Bay.”

Hunt exhaled. He’d survive Delphi’s political labyrinth, but Crete would be war. He gathered the team. “Act III starts now,” he said. “We’ve exposed Nikos and Orlov. Time to cut the snake’s head at Souda Bay and Mykonos.”

Leyla cracked her knuckles. “Finally,” she said. “I was getting tired of galas.” Jenny laughed. “But you looked great in that dress.”

They loaded into vehicles, heading south as the sun set over Delphi. Greece hummed with tension, but the Cedar Coalition’s momentum grew. Hunt glanced at his prayer beads. “Let’s end this,” he whispered.

### 11. Souda Bay Breach
Crete’s Souda Bay naval base sprawled like a sleeping beast—runways, docks, missile batteries. Hunt arrived under cover of a humanitarian supply flight coordinated by Daphne. Captain Stavros Nomikos, commander of Greek SOF, greeted them tersely. “My orders are to assist, not salute,” he said. “You drag ghosts into my bay, I drag them out.”

Hunt nodded. They briefed Nomikos on Lev Orlov’s plan: hide micro-reactor cores inside decommissioned Patriot missile canisters slated for transport to Mykonos. Orlov’s yacht, the *Minotaur*, docked nearby as mobile HQ. Noura and Aris set up a Cedar Coalition lab in an empty hangar, calibrating detectors.

At 0200, Hunt, Leyla, Jenny, Nomikos, and a squad of operators infiltrated the depot. Nomikos insisted on leading. “Greek soil, Greek lead,” he said. They slipped between crates, night-vision painting everything green. Jenny marked suspect canisters with UV paint.

Leyla hacked the manifest terminal, uncovering an unauthorized rail schedule bound for a silo labeled “Aegean Shelter.” Hunt streamed the evidence to Maya. Before they could act, alarms blared—an insider tipped Orlov’s mercs. Gunfire erupted. Hunt and Nomikos fought back-to-back, rifles barking. Leyla’s drone dropped rolling flashbangs to disorient enemies. Jenny yanked a machine gunner off a catwalk with a grappling hook.

Barakat radioed: “Orlov’s yacht is transmitting encrypted bursts.” Noura injected a Cedar Coalition virus, forcing the link offline. Nomikos planted charges on the rail line while Hunt pried open a Patriot canister, revealing a gleaming micro-reactor. He filmed it for Maya. “Proof secured,” he said. They exfiltrated under mortar fire, detonating the rail to bury the remaining canisters.

Nomikos wiped blood from his cheek. “You fight like you belong,” he muttered. Hunt shrugged. “I just hate bullies.” Maya chimed in: “Orlov’s fleeing toward Heraklion. Prep for pursuit.”

### 12. Minotaur’s Data
Orlov retreated to a luxury hotel concealment site codenamed “Labrys.” Daphne traced his crypto wallets to servers hidden beneath the spa. Hunt, Leyla, Jenny, and Yael posed as maintenance staff, wheeling carts full of rifles disguised under towels.

In the server room, Daphne found Orlov’s blockchain keys embedded in 3D scans of Minoan artifacts. She extracted the keys, redirecting funds into a Greek government escrow. “Talal can’t bankroll Maverick now,” she grinned. Hunt planted a worm to mirror Orlov’s comms to Maya. Leyla rigged the cooling system with charges.

Yael intercepted them in the hallway, weapon drawn. “You stole Mossad’s leverage,” she accused. Hunt offered a trade: “Help us nail Orlov, you get first crack at Samir.” She relented. They detonated the cooling units after exiting, frying Orlov’s servers.

Maverick responded with propaganda blaming foreigners for Crete’s economic woes. Protests flared. Nomikos urged them to slip into Heraklion’s old town where Orlov’s convoy would pass. They planned the “Labyrinth Chase.”

### 13. Labyrinth Chase
Heraklion’s Venetian alleys formed a natural ambush. Jenny blocked a drainage canal with her boat, Leyla planted explosives along choke points, Daphne hacked street lights to create blackout zones. Nomikos staged operators disguised as civilians.

When Orlov’s convoy rolled in with drones overhead, Hunt triggered a blackout. Leyla detonated a charge, flipping the lead truck. Orlov’s guards dismounted, confused. Hunt moved through shadows, dropping two men with silent strikes. Jenny fired an EMP slug at the drone swarm, sending them crashing. Barakat picked off stragglers from a rooftop.

Orlov fled on foot. Hunt pursued through the labyrinth, catching him near a frescoed archway. “Cedar Crown is inevitable,” Orlov spat. Hunt answered with a punch, zip-tying him. Nomikos arrested him; Yael fumed but accepted that Greek custody served optics.

Before they could celebrate, remote drones attacked, controlled by Maverick. Jenny used a jury-rigged EMP to fry them; Leyla finished the rest with a hijacked machine gun. Orlov, in chains, screamed about “Phase Two moving to Mykonos.” Hunt smirked. “Thanks for the itinerary.”

### 14. Aegean Lockdown
Maverick sprung another trap. Using hacked base controls, he sealed Hunt, Leyla, Jenny, and Yael inside the decommissioned missile silo nicknamed “Aegean Shelter.” Over the intercom he taunted, “Thirty minutes until steam cooks you alive.”

The silo housed three partially assembled micro-reactors linked to an AI safety system. Leyla hacked furiously; Maverick’s firewall countered. Hunt spotted a maintenance conduit. Jenny volunteered to crawl through, carrying the last EMP pen. She fried the AI controller, rebooting the system into manual mode.

Noura and Aris, outside, guided Jenny via comms to vent steam into reservoirs. Hunt and Leyla used a hydraulic jack to pry open the bulkhead. Yael covered them, ready in case guards rushed in. They burst out as superheated steam roared behind them.

Hunt planted charges to destroy the reactors safely. Maverick snarled over comms, “You can’t save everyone.” Hunt replied, “Watch me.” The silo exploded upward, harmlessly venting into the sky.

### 15. NIS Mutiny
Daphne convened trusted NIS officers in a server room, presenting evidence that Director Gatzos took Talal’s money. Hunt, Leyla, Jenny, Yael, and Nomikos stood guard. Gatzos denied everything until Hunt played audio of him begging Maverick for protection. He drew a pistol; Yael disarmed him in one move. Daphne arrested him, assuming acting directorship.
