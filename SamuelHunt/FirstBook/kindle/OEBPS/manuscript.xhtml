Act I – Smoke over Beirut

1. Hamra Static

Airports smell the same everywhere—burnt coffee, recycled fear—but Beirut’s Rafic Hariri terminal added salt from the Mediterranean and gun oil on the belts of the bored Customs troopers. Samuel Hunt let the smells anchor him. He’d been gone from this part of the world four years, hiding in Jerusalem workshops where he cataloged rare rifles and sabers for a Mossad-adjacent restorer who doubled as his safehouse patron. His passport said he was Nabil Ghali, textile consultant out of Misrata. The alias included fake invoices, a LinkedIn page with exactly eight humblebrag posts, and a wad of Libyan dinars so old they smelled like history. The only thing the cover story couldn’t hide was the way Hunt scanned reflections. Once you’ve counted doors for a living, you never stop.

He moved through Immigration with a tourist slouch. The officer glanced at his documents, looked once at the thin scar under Hunt’s jaw, and stamped him through. A younger officer held up his phone, aimed it toward Hunt. Not a photo. A facial-rec app scraping the crowd for wanted men. Hunt tilted his head to catch the camera’s lens flare, muting his features, then kept walking. Outside, a white Hyundai idled at the curb, its driver holding a cardboard sign that read Ghali in looping Arabic. Hunt ignored it. Too obvious. Instead he rolled his duffel toward the public taxi stand, slipped between two Armenian grandmothers, and whispered the address to Hamra he actually needed.

The driver—khaki vest, nicotine-stained mustache—glanced in the mirror twice. “You from Libya?”

“When it pays,” Hunt said.

They slid through night traffic. Hamra Street shimmered with neon hookah bars, generator fumes, and scooters weaving like they owned gravity. Hunt watched the mirror. Second car back, a black Peugeot tail. Maybe a militia kid playing games; maybe not. Hunt told the driver to stop short of his destination, tipped heavy, and walked the last block. The Peugeot eased past without slowing. Good. He vanished into the side streets, slipped through a half-open metal gate, and took the stairs two at a time.

Leyla Farouq waited in Café al-Tannour, corner booth, back to the wall. She had changed since Mosul—hair shorter, eyes harder. She stirred thick coffee, even though it was midnight. “You quit, ya Sam,” she said. “Burned your badge, swore off ops, went back to cataloging rare weapons with that Israeli restorer contact of yours. What dragged you back?”

Hunt slid a folded photo onto the table. Dr. Noura al-Hadid, smiling in front of a reactor schematic. “She designed a modular core for hospitals. Someone stole her. Ashur Brigades bragged about ‘Cedar Crown’ on a channel Maya flagged yesterday. Even my rare-weapons lifeline heard the whispers.”

Leyla snorted. “Everyone’s heard whispers. Dirty reactor small enough to fit in a shipping container, big enough to blackmail a capital. Your analyst queen wants you to ghost in and out again?” She pushed a jar of Sidon honey toward him. “Bribe for Bedouin checkpoints. They trust sweetness more than paperwork.”

Hunt let the honey catch the light. The jar label read Harvest of 2023 in French script. Everything with Leyla had layers.

“Immigration flagged me,” he said. “Might have a tail already.”

“Then you’ll love this,” she replied. From her tote she produced two badges—one from the Jordan Petroleum Pipeline Authority, another from a fake NGO called Blue Habib Medical Relief. “Pipeline inspector is your primary cover. NGO is your alibi if the pipeline story cracks. I updated your Arabic file. Dialect: Tripolitanian. Favorite food: bamia. These idiots check social feeds before they check passports.”

Hunt thumbed the edges of the badges. “You make heroes out of forged plastic.”

“No,” she said, meeting his gaze. “Heroes make their own myths. I merely invoice them.” She slid a second envelope over. Inside: cash, a prepaid Lebanese SIM, and a metro card for the funicular in case he needed to vanish uphill fast.

Outside, the street vibrated with midnight prayers from a distant mosque and dance music from a rooftop bar. Hunt stepped into it, phone already swapping SIMs. He texted a single punctuation mark to Maya Trask—.—their signal for safe arrival, surveillance probable. Her reply came thirty seconds later: Convoy leaves Wadi Sirhan sector in 19 hours. Find Noura before they cross Syrian line. No support. Long missions used to come with full teams and air cover. Now it was just Hunt, a fixer, and a jar of honey.

He finished the coffee, let the sugar coat his tongue, and felt the old switch flip inside him. Analyst mode became operator mode. Thoughts shorter, sharper. He walked into the humid night, skin prickling, senses stretching. Somewhere above the city a drone hummed. Somewhere beyond the mountains a convoy idled. Hunt squared his shoulders. Beirut had always been his litmus test. If he could move invisibly here, he could disappear anywhere. Tonight, he didn’t want to disappear. He wanted to leave footprints Ashur Brigades couldn’t ignore.

He looped the Corniche twice before heading inland, letting the sea breeze clear his head. Every block he changed pace, altered sidewalk, watched for the Peugeot. Instead of going straight to the safe flat Maya rented, he ducked into an alley hardware shop and bought hose clamps, acetone, and a plastic toolbox tourists would overlook. The owner joked about DIY husbands; Hunt laughed along, paid cash, and left another camera feed filled with a forgettable face. Tradecraft was repetition disguised as coincidence.

Before dawn he climbed to the roof of a shuttered hostel to make his first call to Lieutenant Barakat, the Jordanian officer who owed him a favor. The line stayed silent for five rings before a tired voice answered. “Please tell me this is about soccer,” Barakat muttered.

“Wish it were,” Hunt said. “Need updates on the Wadi Sirhan sector. I’ll owe you another bottle of Arak.”

“You already owe me two,” Barakat replied, but he gave Hunt what he needed—Ashur patrol rosters, the location of a Bedouin sheikh sympathetic to outsiders, and warning that a Syrian militia had started skimming bribes at the Mafraq checkpoint. “Bring honey,” Barakat advised. “And a story better than pipeline maintenance.”

Hunt ended the call, rolled prayer beads between thumb and forefinger, and lay flat against the rooftop to watch the city wake. He cataloged air routes, counted the seconds between drones, noted where generator smoke thinned. Every detail mattered when exits vanished. Only once the sun burned through the morning haze did he finally ghost into the safe apartment, strip his travel clothes, and lay out the kit Leyla provided. The jar of honey sat at the center of the table. Hunt spun it slowly, memorizing the label, the air bubbles, the way it refracted light. Tools were only useful if you knew them by heart.

2. Threads in the Atmosphere

Beirut still hid secrets inside its Ottoman bones. Hunt ducked through a rusted gate, crossed a courtyard choked with jasmine vines, and entered the abandoned Banque du Levant. Dust lay thick on marble floors, glass shards glittered in moonlight, and an old bank vault door yawned open like a hungry mouth. Perfect. Thick walls kept thermal scans from picking him up. The roof had clean sightlines to every diplomatic compound worth eavesdropping on.

He set to work. Copper wire from a stripped transformer. A Raspberry Pi smaller than a cigarette pack. A pair of signal clamps to bite through the insulation on the French consulate’s microwave link. Hunt worked fast, hands steady. He wasn’t the best hacker; never claimed to be. But he knew enough to splice into a signal, skim metadata, and vanish without leaving forensic fingerprints. The Pi booted. A terminal window filled with green characters crawling across black. He piggybacked the embassy uplink and tunneled traffic through a quantum-resistant VPN Maya had built. In Langley, the Directorate would see a ghost user with no origin.

Cargo manifests spilled down the screen—shipments labeled humanitarian aid, water filters, wheat. Hunt filtered for anomalies: refrigerated trucks scheduled to run at subzero temps while carrying blankets. Either some NGO misunderstood insulation, or someone was hiding coolant-dependent tech under tarps. Three trucks popped up. Registration numbers from a Jordanian charity with ties to the Ashur Brigades. Their convoy route: south rim of Wadi Sirhan, three hours from the Syrian badlands. ETA to front gate of pipeline station: nineteen hours. That matched Maya’s warning.

He cross-referenced with a SIGINT feed Trask had piped in, raw intercepts still in Arabic slang. The militants kept calling their cargo al-mu’allimah—the teacher. Noura. Another voice mentioned Shajarat al-Arkan, literally “Guidance Tree.” A code name for the reactor guidance module. Hunt tagged the audio, ran a quick translation overlay, and forwarded the clip to Trask. Her acknowledgment came as a single 👁 emoji. Maya never wasted bandwidth.

A faint vibration ran through the floor. Hunt stilled, ears tuned. Outside, an engine idled. Two cars. Same cadence as the black Peugeot from the airport tail. Maybe coincidence; maybe Ashur scouts. He killed his laptop screen, yanked cables, and poured acetone over the Pi. Plastic smoldered. He crushed the remains with his boot, shoved ashes into an empty coffee tin. No hardware, no evidence.

He slipped through the rear staircase, skipping every third step to avoid creaks. On the roof, the city stretched under him—Hamra’s neon, Gemmayzeh’s dark alleys, the Corniche glittering like a gun barrel. Drones buzzed far off. Hunt crouched beside a crumbling parapet, binoculars up. Two sedans crawled down the street, windows tinted, driver’s posture too upright to be casual. They paused outside the bank gate. One man stepped out, scanned the courtyard, then shrugged and lit a cigarette. Not sure if they were hunting or simply bored. Hunt used the distraction to leap to the adjacent building, cross a laundry line, and drop into a stairwell that smelled of frying onions.

He emerged on a rooftop where Leyla sat atop an AC unit, hair tied back, surveying the skyline. She handed him a radio the size of a thumb drive. “Militia cars sniffing around?” she asked.

“Two,” he said. “Could be nothing.” He briefed her on the convoy intel while plotting his next moves. He would need wheels, forged pipeline documents, and eyes on the convoy before it reached Wadi Sirhan. Leyla listened, eyes narrowed. When he finished, she pointed east. Dawn painted the horizon in bruised reds. “You want the pipeline cover to hold,” she said, “you need grease payments ready. These tribes will let anyone inspect their pipes if they feel respected. Use the honey. Use the dialect I wrote into your file. And for God’s sake, shave. Analysts should look harmless.”

Hunt rubbed the stubble on his chin. She wasn’t wrong. He took a disposable razor from his kit, dry-shaved under the pale light, and watched hair fall like black snow. “One more thing,” he said. “Immigration flagged my alias. We might have a leak.”

Leyla flicked ash from her cigarette. “You’re the leak. A man like you walking Hamra alone? Everyone notices. So keep moving. Keep your texts short. And if you see that Peugeot again, make sure it doesn’t see sunrise.”

He pocketed the radio, secured the honey jar inside his satchel, and headed down the stairs. The sedans had gone. His phone vibrated with another message from Maya: Upload (encrypted clip) RE Cedar Crown. Need your assessment. Hunt typed: Working. No change to mission. He shoved the phone away. Too many moving parts. That was how Cedar Crown wanted it—confusion as camouflage. Hunt breathed in the dawn. The air tasted of diesel and cardamom. Somewhere, Noura waited. He intended to reach her before the convoy did.

He spent the next hour ghosting through Hamra’s back alleys with a handheld spectrum analyzer pressed against concrete walls, mapping out Wi-Fi beacons that Ashur scouts might piggyback on. When he found a strong signal belonging to a defunct travel agency, he planted a repeater disguised as a fuse box. From that perch he could feed video to Maya without touching any infrastructure the Lebanese army monitored. Small redundancies saved lives.

By sunrise his drones were airborne, skimming low over the city. He piloted them from the passenger seat of the Hilux, letting the feed stitch together a mosaic of traffic cameras and alley choke points. One drone captured the black Peugeot parked outside a safehouse rumored to belong to Maverick’s proxies. The car sat empty, engine cold. Hunt tagged the coordinates anyway; patterns mattered. He archived the footage, encrypted it twice, and fired it to Maya with the note: Possible Cedar node. Tag for later burn.

Only then did he allow himself a minute to scrawl in his paper notebook. Route sketches, radio frequencies, the phonetic spelling of Bedouin greetings he’d forgotten. He underlined a single phrase twice—No heroics at checkpoint three. The reminder wasn’t for show. It was for the part of him that thrived on escalation. If he blew his cover before even reaching the desert, Noura wouldn’t get a rescue. He closed the notebook, tucked it under the truck seat, and pointed the Hilux toward the Bekaa.

3. Leyla’s Gambit

Leyla’s legitimate life sat upstairs: framed UNESCO certificates, a reception hall full of glass cases, donors’ names embossed on discreet brass plaques. Downstairs was different. Hunt descended a spiral staircase behind a tapestry and stepped into a catacomb of contraband. Crates of mosaics pried from Syrian ruins. Bronze busts smuggled out of Nineveh. Shelves lined with passports from twenty-one countries. In the center stood Leyla’s workbench, lit by a single gooseneck lamp. Tools arranged like surgical instruments. On the wall, a map of the Levant peppered with pins and yarn.

“Welcome to my honest business,” she said drily. She wore denim and a black scarf, sleeves rolled to the elbow, revealing ink—coordinates of a grave site near Mosul. Hunt had helped her dig there. They never talked about what they’d found.

She slid a tablet across the table. “Pipeline dossier. Names of foremen, bribe preferences, even their favorite soccer clubs. Talk their clubs, they’ll let you tighten valves unsupervised.” She opened a Pelican case. Inside: forged Jordan Petroleum Authority letters with official stamps, heat-laminated badges, and a USB stick containing maintenance logs. “All verified against last month’s leak report,” she said. “You’ll sound legit.”

Hunt pulled on a high-vis vest, scuffed it with boot grease, and clipped the badge to his chest. The vest smelled like cedar and paint thinner. “What’s your endgame, Leyla?” he asked. “We pull this off, what do you want?”

She froze a second too long. “Noura safe,” she said. “Ashur Brigades off my trade routes. And a favor: if you run into a kid named Fareed working with them, get him out. He’s one of my runners. They grabbed him last week moving medicine.”

Illustration: Leyla’s Beirut workshop (contraband wall, pipeline dossier table).

Hunt nodded. Favors layered on favors. He picked up the rifle she’d built for him—a short AR platform with a SOCOM suppressor, night optic, and a sling woven with Paracord and prayer beads. “You kept my specs on file?”

“You’re predictable,” she said. “Short barrel for vehicles, heavy rounds for armor. Here.” She taped two microdrones under the false bottom of his backpack. “Deploy from your phone. Range one kilometer. Camera resolution good enough to read license plates.” She held up a slim silver cylinder. “EMP pen. One burst, short-range. Fries door locks. Don’t waste it.”

Hunt took the cylinder. “And this?” He lifted a paper icon of Saint Barbara, patron of artillerymen, edges singed.

“Insurance,” she said. “Tape it inside your vest. The Bedouin love a soldier who honors saints and prophets alike.”

They worked in companionable silence. Hunt filled jerrycans, checked tire pressure on the rusted Hilux parked in her basement loading dock, synced the drones to his phone. Leyla laid out roads on a chalkboard, tracing the path from Beirut to the Bekaa, across to Mafraq, then down to Wadi Sirhan. “Checkpoints here, here, here. First is Amal. They’ll take cash. Second is a Jordanian Army patrol—flash the pipeline badge, drop a colonel’s name. Third is tribal. That’s where the honey earns you smiles.”

He memorized the map. “What about air surveillance?”

“Ashur hires freelance drone pilots out of Homs,” she said. “They fly Chinese quadcopters at night. You’ll hear them before they see you. Use this jammer.” She tossed a device the size of a paperback. “Battery lasts ten minutes. Save it for when you’re close to the compound.”

Hunt holstered the SIG, slung the rifle, and zipped the backpack. Before he could speak, Leyla placed a hand on his chest. “One more thing,” she said softly. “Don’t die out there. I’m running out of people who owe me drinks.”

He smiled—a small, tired thing. “You keep saving me, we’ll both go broke.” He climbed into the Hilux, turned the key. Diesel coughed, then settled into a steady rumble.

Side Trip – Saint Barbara Cache
Instead of driving straight for the highway, they detoured through an underground souk wedged beneath Hamra’s shuttered cinemas. The Jerusalem Circuit ran the place as a swap meet for orphaned weapon parts. Brother Elias, a Maronite monk who moonlit as quartermaster, rolled butcher paper across a billiards table and revealed Steyr bolts, MAS trigger packs, and bakelite pistol grips stamped with Ottoman crest marks. “Your restorer friend wired ahead,” he said. “Said you’d make it worth our generator bill.” Hunt bartered two gold ottoman coins and a promise of diesel for the monastery in exchange for a cedar box filled with Saint Barbara medallions fused to rifle bolts—tokens the Bedouin respected and customs scanners ignored. Leyla haggled for a spool of MG-42 recoil springs she could recut into drone launchers and a crate of WWII suppressor baffles that, once reamed on Noura’s lathe, would pass as irrigation washers. Mid-negotiation, two Ashur Brigades enforcers slithered through the crowd, still nursing grudges from a Mosul bust Hunt ran five years earlier. Brother Elias tapped a bell; the souk’s hidden shutters slammed shut, forcing the enforcers outside before they could clock Hunt’s face.
“They remember you,” Leyla murmured as they loaded crates. “Maybe getting Saint Barbara’s blessing tonight wasn’t optional.”
“Ashur always remembers,” Hunt said. “Let them chase. We’ve outrun worse ghosts.” By the time they resettled the cache into false-bottom crates, Hunt felt the reassuring weight of history’s spare parts under his seat—and the prickling knowledge that old cases were catching up.

As he pulled out of the underground bay, Leyla jogged alongside, tapped the window. “Sam,” she said, voice suddenly serious. “If you find Maverick, don’t hesitate. Whatever he promised, he’s lying.”

Hunt met her eyes. “I know.” He thought of Caracas, of Maverick dragging a wounded agent out then abandoning him when the exfil bird took too long. Hunt had buried the body. He’d buried a promise that day too. Apparently promises had a way of clawing out of the grave.

He drove toward the ramp that would spit him into Beirut traffic. The muezzin’s dawn call rolled over the rooftops. Behind him, Leyla shut the metal gate, sealing her world again. Ahead, the city opened like a maze. Hunt merged, headlights off, the Hilux blending with delivery trucks and bakery vans. East lay the mountains, then the desert. He hated deserts. Too much sky, not enough cover. But deserts never lied. They either killed you or let you pass. Hunt tightened his grip on the wheel. First checkpoint in eighty minutes. Convoy intercept in eighteen hours. Plenty of time to remember how to be the kind of man the Ashur Brigades feared in their sleep.

He stopped once on the outskirts to rig a false compartment beneath the truck bed, bolting Leyla’s microdrone case next to a hidden med kit. The work took twelve minutes; the peace it bought lasted longer. He cataloged every screw, every bead of sweat freezing against his neck. There was comfort in workmanship. Reacher would have called it superstition. Hunt called it insurance.

Before rolling again, he scrolled through photos of Leyla’s runner Fareed, Noura’s brother Sami, even Barakat’s family. Missions felt less abstract when faces stared back. Cedar Crown wanted to destabilize economies, but it was always the small circles that paid first. Hunt swore that if he survived Wadi Sirhan he’d pay those circles back with interest.

Interlude 01 – Maya Trask Briefing

Tysons Corner, 0315. Fluorescent lights hummed inside the windowless SCIF where Maya Trask lived more than she slept. A half-eaten protein bar perched on a stack of satellite prints. Across the table, Deputy National Security Advisor Sloan rubbed his temples. “You’re telling me one retired MP and a smuggler are all we have between Cedar Crown and a black-market nuke?” he asked.

Maya tapped the screen. Drone footage from Lebanon stitched into a mosaic. “Not retired. Samuel Hunt is asset-neutral. He moves faster without a task force tripping alarms,” she said. “Leyla Farouq provides deniability. Every additional badge we add gives Maverick another camera to hijack.” She swiped to the next slide—thermal imagery of truck convoys crawling toward the Jordanian border. “Phase I is hours from crossing Syria. Phase II is already at sea. We can interdict the second wave if Sam buys us time.”

Sloan leaned back. “State wants plausible deniability. Defense wants strike options. The President wants guarantees.”

“Guarantees are for after-action hearings,” Maya replied. She zoomed in on a blinking dot—the honey jar tracker Leyla embedded. “What I can give you is telemetry, psychological profiles, and the knowledge that Hunt doesn’t quit until the ground refuses him. That’s better than a guarantee.” She slid a folder across the table. “Authorize my SIGINT feeds and the satellite retask. Let me paint the picture in real time.”

Sloan hesitated, then initialed the order. “You get forty-eight hours. After that, this becomes overt.”

When he left, Maya exhaled, alone with humming servers and a muted wanted poster of Maverick rotating on the wall. She sent a single message to Hunt: Sat eyes yours. No cavalry. Then she opened her leather notebook and wrote the names of every civilian living within ten kilometers of the convoy route. If Hunt failed, those were the people she’d have to brief next. Accountability kept her awake more than caffeine ever did.

4. Convoy of Dust

The road east out of Beirut collapsed into switchbacks, then into a cracked ribbon slicing the Bekaa plain. Hunt drove until the city lights died behind him and the sky turned black enough to swallow mistakes. The Hilux’s engine hummed steady. In the passenger seat lay the honey jar, bribe money, and a folded photo of Noura. He glanced at it every thirty kilometers. Not for sentiment—just to burn her face into his brain so no imposter could fool him later.

He hit the first checkpoint outside Baalbek—Amal militia wearing mismatched fatigues, cigarettes dangling. Their leader, a barrel-chested man with a scar that split his eyebrow, rapped on the hood. “Papers,” he said. Hunt handed over the Jordan Pipeline credentials, a letter stamped with the king’s crest, and a folded hundred-dollar bill. The leader scanned the paperwork, flipped the bribe between fingers, and smiled. “Inspector, eh? Pipes leak, politicians leak more. Go on.” He slapped the hood twice. The Hilux rolled on.

Two kilometers later a tow truck blocked the lane. A teenage driver flagged Hunt down, eyes wide. “Radiator cooked,” he said. Hunt should have kept moving; instead he crawled under the truck, bled air, patched the hose with tape, and refilled coolant from one of Leyla’s canisters. Ten minutes lost bought ten years of trust—the convoy behind him reconfigured to shelter the crippled vehicle. As they rolled out again, the teenager pressed a string of prayer beads into Hunt’s palm. “My mother said give this to the man who fixes what isn’t his problem,” he murmured. Hunt tucked the beads beside Saint Barbara. Every charm mattered when bullets started flying.

By midnight Hunt reached the rendezvous coordinates Leyla had sent. A cluster of Bedouin trucks idled beside a stand of scrub acacia, their drivers wrapped in shemaghs, faces lit by campfire glow. A sentinel stepped out, shotgun resting on his shoulder. “Ahlan,” he said cautiously. “You lost?”

“Pipeline inspection,” Hunt answered, switching to Jordanian Arabic. “Supplies for the southern manifold. Heard you run the safest convoy.”

The sentinel snorted. “Who told you that? Leyla? She owes us for the last radiator.” Hunt popped the Hilux’s hood, motioned the man over. “You can have mine,” he said. “Better than talking debt.” He swapped his spare radiator for theirs, hands quick despite cold metal. The Bedouin watched, impressed. “You know engines,” he said. “And verses?” Hunt recited the opening of Sura 91, voice steady. The sentinel’s shoulders loosened. “Ride with us then, Inspector. No one bothers friends of the Qur’an.”

The convoy rolled—six trucks, two pickups, Hunt’s Hilux in the middle. Headlights masked, speed steady, radios whispering old hymns. Hunt counted silhouettes atop dunes, noted heat blooms through night-vision glasses. Twice he caught glints—snipers? He logged coordinates in his head case. Every detail mattered later.

They paused at a dry cistern to swap drivers. Bedouin grandmothers ladled sweet tea from dented kettles, insisting the “Inspector” drink. Hunt knelt on the sand with them and listened as they recited folklore about spirits that guarded hidden wells. One elder traced circles in the sand, warning him that Ashur scouts bribed children with satellite phones. “If a boy waves two hands at you, it means he has a GPS tracker in his pocket,” she said. Hunt filed it away. Intelligence wore a thousand faces—tonight, it wore henna.

Illustration: Wadi Sirhan convoy route (Amal checkpoint → Atlas approach).

An hour in, a flash of green in the sky. Drone. Hunt heard the high-pitched whine. He slowed, reached under his seat, and pulled the jammer Leyla had given him. He clipped it to a diesel drum in the truck ahead, powered it on. The drone feed hiccupped. On the militants’ screens, his convoy would look like farmers hauling irrigation pumps. Ten minutes later the battery died, but by then they’d faded into wadi shadows.

At the Jordan border, an army checkpoint loomed—floodlights, razor wire, sandbag towers. Lieutenant with perfect posture waved them down. Hunt rolled forward, flashed the pipeline badge, dropped a colonel’s name Leyla had prepped. “Inspection orders,” he said in English textured with Tripolitanian vowels. The lieutenant checked the paperwork, then smiled. “Welcome back, sir,” he said, accent American. “We served together in Mosul.” Hunt blinked. Recognition clicked. This was Amjad “AJ” Faris, a Jordanian liaison who’d once covered Hunt’s flank in Nineveh. AJ leaned close. “Saw your face on a watchlist from Damascus,” he whispered. “Play it cool. I’ll scrub the entry log.” He slapped the hood theatrically. “Next time bring better coffee, Inspector.” Hunt nodded once. Old war debts still paid dividends.

Beyond the border, desert swallowed them whole. The trucks left asphalt, cut south along a dry riverbed. Sandstorm ghosts flitted across their path. Drivers wrapped scarves tighter, radios now muttering prayers. Hunt watched the horizon. Far off, sodium lights glowed—Ashur Brigades’ forward base. He felt the desert’s old dread crawl under his skin. Too much sky, no cover. He forced his breathing steady, counted eight beats in, eight beats out. Dust coated his teeth. Sweat glued his shirt to his back despite the cold.

They halted in a gulch two kilometers short of the compound. Hunt climbed out, stretched, and shook hands with the driver beside him—a man named Tarek with laugh lines and eyes hardened by famine. “We stop here,” Tarek said. “Ashur patrols the next ridge. They like to shoot at headlights.” Hunt nodded, handed him the honey jar. “For your boys,” he said. Tarek accepted, touched his heart. “Come back alive, Inspector. Radiators safe with us.”

Hunt made final prep. He swapped plates on the Hilux, stashed the microdrones in his vest, checked weapon load. He cached extra magazines under a rock outcrop, marked the spot with a broken bottle. Then he palmed the Saint Barbara icon taped inside his vest, whispered an almost forgotten prayer, and began the hike. Sand shifted under boots. A cold wind knifed through his jacket. Ahead, the Shahrazad plant’s glow pulsed like a lighthouse guiding him toward hell. Hunt adjusted his sling, lowered into a crouch, and disappeared into the dunes.

5. Perimeter Equation

The Shahrazad complex rose from the wadi like a mirage: prefab walls, perimeter lights, a forest of antenna masts clawing at the sky. Hunt lay prone behind a ridge of basalt, night-vision goggles pulsing green. He counted breaths, counted guards, counted seconds between floodlight sweeps. Soldier math kept nerves from fraying.

He released the first microdrone. It buzzed low, hugging sand, then rose to perch on a floodlight housing. Video fed straight to his phone. He pinched to zoom. Inside the yard, three KAMAZ trucks sat under camouflage tarps. Their thermal signature screamed “cold cargo.” That meant coolant, not blankets. Good intel cross-check.

Next, he mapped the mortar pit, the ammo dump, and the guard towers. Twenty-eight fighters visible. Add ten more for interior shifts. He noted a trailer with Iranian call signs—a signals hut. He would have to knock that out first or risk Ashur calling Tehran mid-raid.

He dialed Maya on the encrypted radio. “Compound confirmed,” he whispered, voice barely a breath. “Visual on Dr. al-Hadid. Request sat cover 0400 to 0600.”

Static. Then Maya’s voice, all steel. “Denied. Turkish radar up, Russian AWACS overhead. You’re the coverage.”

Translation: no drones, no eavesdropping, no cavalry. Hunt grunted. “Copy. Going dark.” He slid the radio away. Alone it was, then.

He shifted the drone feed to thermal. In the prefab lab, a lone figure moved—small, hair tucked under a cap, shoulders hunched. Noura. She paced between coolant manifolds, hands shaking. Hunt focused the optic, mentally rehearsing the route to her building. He visualized guard paths, counted steps: from gate to lab, 120 meters. With a cover story, he might walk it. Without, he’d crawl.

He scribbled a sketch in his field notebook. Gate. Fuel depot. Signals trailer. Lab. Mortar pit. He marked probable sniper nests—higher dunes with good sightlines. He also spotted something new: a shipping container half-buried near the perimeter. Through IR, he saw heat plumes rising. Could be power cells. Could be the backup core. Act III – Operation Cedar Shield

For the sabotage he unscrewed the jar, smeared the viscous gel into the intake strainers, then triggered the chemical activator. He kicked aft, planting the second beacon near the rudder shaft. On the way back he caught a glimpse of a Syrian sailor leaning over the rail, cigarette ember arcing toward the sea. Hunt froze beneath the surface until the ember hissed out. When the sailor moved away, Hunt surfaced behind the Artemis Dawn, lungs burning. Leyla hauled him aboard, wrapped him in an old army blanket, and shoved a mug of bracing tea into his hands.

“Washington always shrugs,” Hunt muttered. He checked his watch. “Three hours until the gel hardens. Rotate rest.”

Leyla rolled her eyes but took the helm while he catnapped against a coil of rope, rifle across his chest, headphones piping in Maya’s SIGINT feed. He dreamed of sandstorms colliding with waves, of Maverick’s laugh echoing through cargo holds. When he jolted awake, dawn smeared pink along the horizon. Ahead, the Aswad Star shuddered. Its engines coughed, slowed, then died entirely. The ship drifted broadside to the swell, crew voices rising in panic. Leyla throttled up.

“Alarm is ours,” Hunt said, slinging his rifle. “Time to board.”

12. Boarding Weather

The Mediterranean turned slate gray as the sun climbed, wind whipping white caps across the stalled convoy. The Aswad Star wallowed, engines choking against crystallized sugar. On the horizon, the “fishing trawler” Borey-12 arrowed closer—gray hull, radar mast bristling with dish antennas, flank stenciled with Cyrillic numbers that VolgaShield had halfheartedly painted over. Its AIS insisted it was a harmless longliner. Its deck guns disagreed.

Hunt powered up the spear drone Leyla had liberated from a French arms depot. The carbon-fiber dart hummed as it spooled off the launcher, towing a monofilament line. He launched it toward the Aswad’s stern. On his tablet he watched the drone skim inches above the water, dive under the hull, and slice through the rudder cables. Metal pinged. The Aswad yawed, unable to steer. Syrian crewmen cursed, leaning over the rail to see what phantom bit them.

Leyla popped two smoke buoys, turning the water between the Artemis Dawn and the Aswad into a milky fog. “Forty-five seconds before it thins,” she warned.

Hunt hooked a grappling line, swung aboard, and landed cat-quiet beside a stack of relief boxes. He wore the humanitarian vest Leyla tailored—Jordanian patch sewn crooked, for authenticity. MPX up, he moved through the confusion. A Syrian guard raised his rifle, eyes wide. Hunt shot him in the shoulder, letting him live. “Inspection,” he barked in Arabic. “Your compassion paperwork smells like diesel.”

He advanced, rifle low, dispatching guards with butt strokes that left them unconscious but breathing. He didn’t need more bodies tonight. What he needed lay below deck, where he heard muffled yelling. He kicked open a hatch, descended into a compartment reeking of rust and ammonia.

“They can call us pirates,” Hunt said. “We’ve been called worse.” He slid down beside Leyla, letting the Mediterranean spray salt his wounds. Ahead, the horizon softened. Behind, Cedar Crown’s maritime arm smoldered. For a moment, Act III belonged to them.

13. Thermite Sea

Flames chewed through the crates like termites through cedar. Each thermite puck Noura had smuggled out of Shahrazad burned white-hot, molten metal dripping onto the deck. Hunt crouched behind the Aswad’s wheelhouse, scanning past the heat shimmer. Two VolgaShield skiffs zigzagged closer, PKM gunners braced. Their captain likely assumed Hunt would never dare fire on Russians. Hunt liked disappointing assumptions.

He hefted the captured Igla launcher he’d yanked from a stunned Ashur guard. Instead of aiming at the trawler, he locked onto the seawater ahead of its bow. He fired. The missile screamed low, slammed into the waves twenty meters in front of the Borey-12, and detonated. A geyser of water and shrapnel rose, peppering the hull. The message was simple: next one hits steel. The skiffs wobbled. No VolgaShield contractor wanted to explain a dead American to Moscow.

He keyed his throat mic. “Yara, give me vectors,” he whispered. In Tripoli, the hacker piped satellite overlays into his HUD, painting wind arrows along the swells and tagging every AIS blip inside five nautical miles. Leyla flooded the Aswad’s VHF with Bedouin pop ballads so any Mayday would arrive garbled, then spun up her palm-sized ECM puck to fuzz the Borey-12’s fire-control radar. Hunt felt the hair on his arms rise as the jammers hissed—proof that their ad-hoc EW kit held for the moment.

Above, two Turkish F-16s roared past, afterburners painting the sky orange. Officially a training sortie; unofficially a warning. Maya had retasked them faster than bureaucracy should allow. Hunt waved at the trawler, mouthed, “Back off.” After a beat, the captain ordered reverse. Engines churned. The skiffs peeled away, still aiming guns but keeping distance.

Hunt tossed the empty launcher aside, grabbed Barakat under the arms, and dragged him toward the rail. The lieutenant’s coughs rattled. “You always dramatize extractions,” he wheezed.

“You always need rescuing,” Hunt shot back. He looped a line under Barakat’s torso, shoved him overboard, and dove after. Mediterranean salt burned every scrape. He swam hard toward the Artemis Dawn, where Leyla leaned over with a rope ladder.

14. Ghost Debrief

They disappeared into Gemmayzeh, up a staircase hidden behind a shuttered French bookstore whose proprietor owed Leyla three favors. Dust motes floated in the stale air. The smell of salt, ink, and ozone clung to the walls. Hunt, Leyla, Noura, and Barakat huddled around a flickering holo projector projecting Maya Trask’s face from a vault somewhere beneath Langley. Samir sat bound in a corner, gag removed so he could answer questions if needed.

“You melted their guidance package,” Maya began without pleasantries. “Good. But Maverick countered with a manifesto. Video’s already on twelve networks. Maya scowled. “Focus. Cedar Crown’s financing line runs through Prince Talal bin Masri. He’s laundering money via a Cypriot shelf called Trident Holdings. Hunt bit back a retort. “Fair,” he conceded. “But if one coin lands in Talal’s wallet, I’ll hang it around your neck.”

Noura, wrapped in a gray blanket, cleared her throat. “What about Samir?” she asked softly. Exhaustion hollowed her eyes. “He saved me twice. He also betrayed everyone twice.”

“We’ll set up a lab in Limassol,” Hunt promised. “Leyla, reach out to Elena. Barakat, you escort Noura and babysit Samir. I’ll coordinate flights and scrub the manifests Maverick leaked.”

Leyla flicked open a holo-map of the Cypriot freeport, its bonded warehouses color-coded. “Pier 12 has blind CCTV for thirty seconds whenever the cruise ferries dock,” she said. “Elena can park us there, but we need cover paperwork—antique weapons restoration exhibit, maybe?” Hunt nodded, already drafting a manifest that mixed genuine artifacts from his Jerusalem stash with hollow crates for kit. Barakat listed sling-load weights for Noura’s lab gear, muttering about customs inspectors who measured everything twice.

Samir watched quietly, eyes calculating. Hunt noticed and leaned in. “Memorize every map,” he warned. “If you run, I’ll post them to Mossad’s holiday mailing list.” Samir looked away first.

Samir scoffed. “You can’t keep me forever.”

Hunt crouched so their eyes were level. “No. One day you’ll walk free. But when you do, Mossad, Riyadh, Tehran, Langley, and Maverick will all know you betrayed them. That’s not freedom. That’s a death sentence served slow.”

Samir swallowed.

Maya cut the feed. Static filled the room. Outside, Beirut traffic blared, ordinary and oblivious. Hunt rubbed his eyes. “We move at 0400,” he said. “Cyprus first, Tripoli after. Maverick will drop another video before sunset; let him. We’ll give the world evidence.”

Leyla stretched, spine popping. “Fifty-two hours awake,” she muttered. “Guinness should issue a certificate.”

“Sleep on the charter plane,” Hunt said. “Bring your shotgun; Cypriot customs love confiscating souvenirs.” He walked to the shuttered balcony, watched two teenagers argue about soccer. He smiled despite himself. The world spun, unaware of the thermite still cooling in the Mediterranean. He intended to keep it spinning.

15. Fault Lines

46. Mykonos Debrief

Days later, the coalition finally made it to Mykonos. Leyla insisted on a proper Greek thank-you: a long table at a taverna in Ano Mera, whitewashed walls, blue shutters, and a view of the Aegean that looked almost innocent. Noura clinked glasses with him. “And to the students who’ll never know how close they came to studying radioactive fallout,” she added.

Barakat tapped his glass loudly. “I have something,” he announced. “We’re officially naming the dogs honorary members of the Hellenic Coast Guard.” He produced tiny dog-sized life jackets embroidered with Greek flags. Athena barked; Thanos knocked over a wine glass with his tail.

Saint Barbara’s icon sat in the center of the table, propped against a bottle of ouzo. When the bells of the nearby church chimed midnight, Hunt touched the icon and whispered, “We’ll be ready.”

47. Stillness in Syros

For once, they actually took a few days off as promised. The coalition rented a cluster of stone cottages on Syros, quieter than Mykonos but close enough for support if the world exploded again. Leyla spent day one asleep on a terrace lounger, a straw hat over her face, Athena curled against her legs. Noura joined local fishermen at dawn, relearning the patience of casting nets. Barakat explored the island’s tunnels with Eleni, cataloging WWII graffiti for the arsenal archives. Yael disappeared into an old record shop and emerged with vinyls she insisted would soothe even Spartan commandos.

Hunt tried to rest. He hiked the hill above Ermoupoli, overlooking the harbor. For the first hour he counted sails instead of threats. For the second hour he answered old voicemails from Maya, Tarek, and his Jerusalem contacts, each message ending with “rest” or “drink water.” On day three, he finally did nothing—sat on a rock, ate pistachios, watched a ferry depart, and let the Aegean wind erase the adrenaline.

On their final night, they gathered on the beach, lighting a bonfire with driftwood. Leyla played Greek folk songs on a borrowed bouzouki, badly but enthusiastically. Tarek roasted lamb over the flames, declaring it inferior to Jordanian mansaf but acceptable. Katerina brought bottles of island wine and jars of pistachio spoon sweet.

“This will last until the next crisis,” Leyla said, raising her cup.

“Then we load pistachios instead of reactors,” Hunt replied.

Athena and Thanos sprinted along the shoreline, chasing gulls. The sea glowed with bioluminescence under their paws, trailing blue fire. Noura leaned against the bonfire and whispered, “If we ever build a fusion lab in Greece, it’ll look like this.”

“Just add better music,” Yael teased.

They stayed until the embers died. When dawn came, Hunt packed Saint Barbara’s icon, rolled up his kit, and felt—for the first time in months—like he’d actually rested. The war would return. Cedar Crown’s remnants would mutate. But the few quiet days on Syros proved they could pause without forgetting why they fought.
